Preview

Africa has some of the poorest telecommunications markets in the world. Many nations of Africa have struggled with poor economies, lack of investment, spotty telecommunications infrastructure, political instability, and war. Although Africa is now considered the fastest growing telecom sector in the world, it has a lot of work to do before it catches up to the rest of the world, and its ability to continue to grow hinges heavily on international or private investment. Like many parts of the developing world, mobile technologies have helped bring telephone service and Internet access to people who could not afford a landline or do not have access to one. However, the overall mobile adoption rates still trail those found in other parts of the world with similar infrastructure and economic problems, such as the Caribbean or the Middle East.

Executive Summary

[return to top of this report]

Weak fixed-line infrastructure and overall low penetration rates allow significant room for industry expansion in Africa. Plans are in progress for installing fiber networks under the ocean to reach Africa, as well as to build and implement satellite connectivity, as African leaders recognize the need to improve Internet availability. Many foreign investors are collaborating to invest significant funds in building telecommunications infrastructure in Africa, recognizing the huge potential for revenue. In general, there is a growing trend toward combining and merging companies to enhance strengths and reduce competition within mobile telephony markets. Worldwide telecom companies are now turning their eyes to Africa as a source of potential growth and investment, which is good news for those seeking new services like broadband, WiMAX, and VoIP.

The most impressive figures are to be found in the mobile sector, which has seen an explosion in growth. Cellular access across the continent has experienced a compound annual growth rate of nearly 50 percent from 2002 to 2007, and 93 percent of Africa's phones are now mobile. Growth in many countries has exceeded an average of 100 percent per year. Nonetheless, Africa is significantly behind the world average in mobile penetration (76 percent globally), with penetration rates on the continent ranging from 1 percent to 89 percent, and mainly in the less than 20 percent bracket. As is the case in other parts of the world, particularly in Eastern Europe, the rate of development in Africa varies significantly from country to country. For instance, although Nigeria, Cameroon, Algeria, Libya, Kenya, Angola, and Congo have a high rate of growth in mobile access - in some cases because they started with nothing - Mauritius, Guinea, and South Africa are at the lower end of the spectrum - partly due to a more mature market. Recent civil unrest, wars, famine, and drought have swept the central regions, further decreasing prospects for infrastructure improvement.

Internet access on the continent also has increased, but the statistics can be deceiving. Although Internet access is now available in virtually all capital cities in Africa, there are many shared Internet accounts, and a lot of inhabitants make use of public access services, so coming up with accurate Internet penetration rates has been a challenge. Overall, Internet penetration is about 9.6 percent, with a range from one percent in Tanzania to 26 percent in Mauritus.

As African countries continue to reform their telecom sectors, governments are encouraging foreign investment as a means of facilitating the introduction of privatization and liberalization. Key to these developments will be the willingness of dominant market leaders to invest in infrastructure and roll out new products and services, and these leaders will be in the unique position to take advantage of the market trends currently at play.

One issue of importance to this market is high taxation and unreliable electricity supplies. Currently, consumers in East Africa pay 25 to 30 percent taxes on mobile phone services, whereas the rest of Africa pays an average of 17 percent. Tanzania and the Democratic Republic of Congo are the only countries in Africa that still impose a customs duty on imported mobile handsets, making the cost of a cell phone prohibitive for many individuals. Prepaid offerings, liberalization of telecom companies, and rollout of 3G services are major contributors to the growth of mobile services within Africa.

Market Dynamics

[return to top of this report]

Advancements aside, Africa still has the world's lowest fixed-line penetration rates, so basic telephony is still a necessity in many countries on the continent. Evan during the global recession, Africa's telecom sector continued to report an increase in the number of mobile phone subscribers and data usage. In Africa, wireless competition is slim; the mobile sector is open to competition in two-thirds of the countries, with between two and five operators. The East Africa Submarine System (EASSy) and the East Africa Marine system (TEAMs) cables will result in affordable bandwidth that could open up the market for cheaper connectivity. Africa will experience increased consolidation and mergers in an attempt to compete for access within these lucrative markets. Several countries have not yet privatized the telecommunications industry, despite promises over the years to do so. In markets that have been both privatized and deregulated, there has been exponential growth, increased competition, reduced rates to clients, and expansion in the amount and quality of services offered, including broadband and triple-play packages.

Most of the world's largest telecom and IT firms have offices in Africa, including Alcatel, Compaq, Ericsson, Vodafone, Samsung Electronics, Nortel Networks, Nokia, Shanghai Bell Company, ZTE, Mobile Telephone Networks, Marconi, and Italtel. Global Services has established a B2B service that matches buyers and sellers of telecom network equipment and assists African nations in building their communications infrastructures.

There are several important drivers in this sector, as follows:

  • The need to extend telecommunications services into rural areas. Currently, there are some countries in which there is a single telephone for 1,000 people or more in the rural sector. This fact is largely masked by teledensity figures. Most of the recent spectacular growth in mobile services has been confined to the cities due to the size and relative wealth of the market. One critical issue for rural deployment is that, although fixed-line infrastructure and subscriptions are relatively high, mobile handset costs and per-minute tariffs are often even higher and can be out of reach of rural users. For this reason, there is a general movement away from the idea of "universal service" to one of "universal access." Universal access is being provided through public telecenters subsidized by the government or by the local telcos. These range from simple payphones, to re-purposed shipping containers with several telephones and sometimes Internet access facilities.
  • The need to improve Internet access. Access to information is seen as crucial for development, and countries in this area are eager to join the information age. Internet penetration rates remain very low; however, there are programs to improve access, particularly through public kiosks and telecenters. But bandwidth remains an issue for most of the continent, including at major academic and research institutions.
  • Spectacular growth in the mobile sector. The area has seen tremendous growth of cellular services, which do not require the capital outlay required for fixed-line infrastructure. Cellular or wireless access is also more appropriate to the inhospitable terrain and widespread villages of these countries. Much of the mobile growth remains within the cities, but it is bringing tremendous benefits to the region. There is some 2.5 and 3G service being planned, but it is not likely to occur for some time, since the main focus is on expanding access.
  • Commitment to Universal Access, through provision of telecenters, which may be either wireless or fixed line. This is seen as the solution to bringing communications to all areas of the continent. Many of these schemes are also designed to be run as small businesses and stimulate local economies.

There are numerous local and international aid schemes funneling money into Africa's telecommunications infrastructure, and outside investment is also critical to growth. With the extremely high rate of development in some sectors, there is plenty of room for investment. Investors need to consider the long-haul, however, since immediate returns are likely to be slim. It is also important to note that there are extensive differences between the Arab north, central "sub-Saharan" region, and the ex-colonial breadbaskets of South Africa and Zimbabwe. Each area faces unique problems. Civil strife, war, and famine have swept through the region, and all have had an effect upon infrastructure and investment.

Major market conditions differ widely. Some of these markets and recent developments are discussed below.

Algeria

Located in North Africa, Algeria is geographically the largest country in Africa and the tenth largest in the world. The telecom market in Algeria is generally healthier than in most countries of Africa, but it is still poor compared to Western standards. The incumbent service provider, Algeria Telecom, was a monopoly until 2005 when the government issued a license to a second player called Consortium Algerien des Telecommunication (CAT). This second provider has brought only limited competition, and the fixed-line density rate is just eight percent. The government announced in late 2013 that it is interested in selling part of its stake in Algeria Telecom and several other nationalized companies on the stock market.

Algeria has 37.7 million mobile customers which means 98 percent of the population has wireless telephones. There are three companies competing there: Algeria Telecom, Global Telecom, and Wataniya Telecom Algeria. All three have GSM networks and licenses to offer 3G service, although next-generation service is only available in a few markets.

Algeria has a broadband penetration rate of just 2.9 percent, but that is one of the highest in Africa. In order to promote faster Internet connections and prevent the theft of copper networking cable, Algeria Telecom is upgrading its infrastructure to fiber-optic lines. In 2013, it also rolled out a nationwide fiber-optic backbone network.  

Angola

Angola is a major oil producer and one of the wealthier countries in the region, but the country was torn apart by decades of civil war that finally ended in 2002. As a result, less than 1.5 percent of the population has a fixed-line telephone. The fixed-line market was opened to competition in 2005, however, and there are four other companies operating in this sector. As the country continues to recover, Angola Telecom continues to invest in expanding its network into more rural and remote parts of the country.

Angola has 9.8 million wireless customers, a 47 percent density. Angola Telecom and Unitel are the two providers. Unitel is currently conducting trials of an LTE network and made the first 4G video call in Africa.

There are just 31,300 broadband customers in Angola (0.2 percent of the population), but Telegeography reports that Africa is going to lead the world in terms of demand for more international bandwidth between 2012 and 2019, and Angola is leading the way. The country's bandwidth demands are expected to grow by 71 percent annually over the next seven years. Angola Telecom is majority owner in a company called Angola Cables that is building a submarine cable connection to Brazil. Once this $200 million project is complete in mid-2015 it will provide the country with substantially more bandwidth. Angola Cables also plans to build out a terrestrial connection to Zambia and Tanzania by 2016. 

Benin

Benin's telecommunications market has been hampered by a lack of infrastructure. According to the most recent information published by the International Telecommunications Union, there are just 156,715 landlines servicing a population of 10 million people, meaning only 1.5 percent of the population has a landline telephone. Benin Telecoms has a monopoly on all local and long distance services, but the wireless sector is open to competition.

The wireless density rate is 84 percent, as the people use mobile technologies for basic telephone service since landlines are so hard to come by. There are five carriers operating in the region: MTN, Bell Benin Communications, Libercom, Globacomm, and Moov. MTN Benin offers 3G service and has a license to operate a 4G LTE network.

The broadband sector is also very poor, with only 0.05 percent of the people having a high-speed connection. Connections are expensive, but in 2012 the country received its third submarine cable connection which is expected to help drive down prices.

Botswana

Botswana has historically been a wealthy nation by African standards as a result of the diamond exploration and mining there. It boasts a highly competitive telecommunications market and initiated steps to full privatization in 2006 and 2007. The country has experienced strong growth in the mobile and broadband services markets but a consistent reduction in the number of fixed lines. All telecommunications systems are fully digital with fiber optic cables linking the major population centers in the east. Botswana Telecommunications Corporation (BTC) is the primary fixed-line provider, and the government is preparing to privatize it.

Botswana has a fixed-line teledensity of eight percent, which is high for a region where several countries are less than one percent. Botswana is also among the highest in the region in terms of mobile penetration, which is currently 156 percent. There are three wireless companies operating in the country: Botswana Telecommunications Corporation (BTC), Orange, and MTN. Orange and MTN both offer 3G services.

The broadband penetration rate is just 0.9 percent, but the government is hoping that competition will push the market. In preparation of privatizing BTC, the government split off its infrastructure division into a separate wholesale provider. It also awarded a license to Botswana Power Corporation (BPC) that allows it to use its fiber-optic cable infrastructure to provide telecommunications service.

Burkina Faso

Landlocked Burkina Faso is among the poorest countries in the world. The per-capita GDP is just $634 per year and 90 percent of the population engaged in subsistence agriculture. The country also has a poor telecommunications sector. The fixed-line density rate is just 0.9 percent, and the main fixed-line provider is a company called Onatel, which is a subsidiary of Maroc Telecom. In 2013, Maroc Telecom was subject to several rumors that it was going to be acquired.

There are 10 million mobile customers, which puts the density rate at 61 percent. There are three companies competing in this sector: Onatel, Airtel, and Telecel Faso.

The broadband sector is virtually non-existent. Only 0.09 percent of the population have high-speed connections.

Burundi

Burundi is one of the poorest nations in the world and thus has one of the least developed telecommunications markets. Less than 0.2 percent of the population has a landline telephone, and Onatel Burundi holds a monopoly over the market so there is little incentive to improve its network. In April 2013, the federal government said it is interested in selling a majority stake in the incumbent with the understanding that the new owner will upgrade the country's infrastructure.

In situations where a country has such a poor fixed-line infrastructure, the mobile market is usually thriving, but that is not the case here. The wireless density rate is just 23 percent, even though there is competition. The following companies operate in the market: Africell, Econet, Office National des Telecommunications, Lacell, and Telecel-Burundi.

The broadband sector is non-existent. There are just 487 high-speed subscribers in a country of almost 9.9 million people.

Cameroon

Cameroon's telecommunications sector has undergone considerable transformation since privatization of the mobile subsidiary of the national operator, Cameroon Telecommunications Corporation (Camtel). Mobile penetration has increased dramatically from nine percent in 2005 to over 60 percent at the end of 2012. Today, there are three wireless companies operating in the sector: Camtel, MTN, and SCM.

While the mobile sector has seen strong growth, the fixed-line infrastructure is old and outdated. Only 3.4 percent of the population has a landline telephone, and government plans to privatize Camtel's fixed-line business has failed several times.

Cameroon has a broadband penetration rate of just 0.2 percent. The operators are trying to generate new revenue streams from the virtually untapped Internet and broadband market by introducing mobile data and WiMAX wireless broadband services. Camtel has been allowed to monopolize access to the SAT-3/WASC international fiber optic submarine cable, which has led to extremely high prices and a grey market of unlicensed satellite gateway operators offering Internet access and Voice over Internet Protocol (VoIP) services. In November 2013, Cameroon secured $6.2 million in funding to expand a nationwide fiber-optic network across the country.

Cape Verde

Cape Verde is a series of islands off the western coast of Africa. Home to 494,000 people, it is one of the wealthier nations of Africa with a per-capita GDP of $4,695. The International Telecommunications Union has ranked it as the least developed country with the highest telephone density since 1996. At the end of 2012, it had a fixed-line density of 14 percent, a wireless penetration rate of 86 percent, and a broadband rate of four percent, all of which are high for the region.

Cabo Verde Telecom is the primary fixed-line operator. It has a fiber-optic ring connecting all islands of the archipelago, and it has a submarine cable connection to Europe, Senegal, and South America. It and T-Mobile are the mobile providers.

Central African Republic

The Central African Republic has one of the poorest telecom sectors in Africa. There are only 823 local access points in service, so just 0.02 percent of the people have a landline telephone, and there are no broadband connections. The Societe Centrafricaine des Telecommunications (SOCATEL) is the main provider.

Countries with poor telecom infrastructure typically have high wireless densities, but the Central African Republic has a mobile density rate of only 25 percent. The four wireless providers are Moov, NationLink Telecom RCA, Orange, and Telecel Centrafrique.

Chad

Chad has a history of poverty, civil war, disease, drought, and famine. The country ranks as one of the most corrupt government leaderships in Africa. It lags behind its neighbors on almost all telecommunications figures with high costs and low teledensity rates in all market sectors, far below African averages. There are only 19,800 fixed lines in the country, a density rate of 0.2 percent. The wireless density rate (35 percent) and broadband rate (0.16 percent) are also poor. However, the recent discovery of oil there has analysts seeing a brighter future.

There are three mobile companies providing service to the country's 4.4 million mobile customers: Airtel, Millicom, and Salam.

Chad managed to propose a detailed national ICT plan in late 2007. The plan, which is similar to those of other African nations, proposed a series of collaborations between private and public sectors to help improve the broadband sector. In September 2013, the government announced it was looking for partners to help it build a national fiber-optic network that will connect the capital city of N'Djamena with the Central African Republic, Niger, Nigeria, and the Sudan.

Cote D'Ivoire

The telecom sector within Cote D'Ivoire has undergone privatization and liberalization. The civil war that began in 1999 and lasted until the signing of the peace agreement in the spring of 2007 disrupted growth but did not stop it; however, civil unrest in early 2011 following disputed presidential elections led to a contraction of the economy. GDP from 2012 on is expected to return to about 6 percent growth annually. Cote d'Ivorie Telecom provides most of the fixed-lines services. France Telecom owns 51 percent of the company, and in December 2013 the federal government announced plans to sell off another 28 percent of the company for about $9 million.

In the mobile sector, there are six companies providing service to the 18.1 million customers: Cafe, Comium, GreenN, Moov, MTN Cote d'Ivoire, and Orange.

There are no broadband connections.

Democratic Republic of the Congo

The Democratic Republic of the Congo (formerly Zaire) is a relatively wealthy country because of its mineral deposits, but it struggled with 30 years of dictatorial rule and decades of civil strife. The telecommunications sector is unusual, lacking any real national regulatory policies. Landline services are offered by SCPT which theoretically has a monopoly, but the government only loosely polices the market. The country has been unable to provide a modern telecom network and has been unsuccessful in finding major investors for privatization. Mobile services have flourished under this regime, but the fixed network has deteriorated to almost non-existence. There are just 59,500 fixed lines which equates to a density rate of 0.09 percent.

The mobile sector has seen triple-digit growth almost every year since 2000, rising from less than 8,000 subscribers in 1996 to 20.1 million today (a density of 31 percent). At one point, 16 companies had licenses to provide mobile service in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, but the explosive growth and the large number of carriers led to problems with spectrum shortages and interference. The market eventually went through a period of consolidation and there are now six companies: Airtel, Orange, Congolese Wireless Network, SuperCell, Tigo, and Vodacomt. The vast majority of subscribers are on prepaid plans.

Development of the Internet market has been held back by the poorly developed national and international infrastructure. Mobile operators are playing a strong role in this sector after the launch of mobile data services over Wi-MAX and 3G technologies. With support from China, SCPT is now finally rolling out a fiber optic national backbone that will ultimately also provide access to fiber optic submarine cables for low-cost, high-quality international bandwidth. In 2013, the country connected to a submarine fiber-optic cable that gave it additional bandwidth.

Djibouti

Djibouti is a small country on the east coast of Africa that borders Eritrea, Ethiopia, Somalia, the Red Sea, and the Gulf of Aden. Covering less than 9,000 square miles, it is one of the smaller countries on the continent. Djibouti Telecom has a monopoly on the telecom sector, which is poor. The penetration rates for fixed-lines, mobile service, and broadband are 2.3 percent, 25 percent, and 1.7 percent, respectively. In July 2013, the World Bank criticized the country for allowing Djibouti Telecom to continue to hold a monopoly on the market, claiming that the $36 per month it charges for 1M bps DSL speed amounts to half of what an average citizen makes in a year.

Egypt

The Egyptian telecom sector has performed consistently well, which is mainly a result of its competitive policies in all markets. In spite of political and social unrest since 2011, Egypt is one of the most developed Internet markets in Africa in terms of users, international bandwidth, and services offered. Growth in both fixed-line and mobile communications is excellent. Privatization of the incumbent Telecom Egypt occurred in 2005, and its fixed-line monopoly expired in 2006. Today, Egypt has the largest fixed-line market in Africa. There are 8.6 million local access lines and the density rate is 10.6 percent, which is one of the highest on the continent.

It was one of the first countries in Africa to launch 3G mobile services and has seen an annual growth in mobile broadband subscriptions of more than 200 percent. There are now 96.7 million wireless customers, a 120 percent density rate. There are three companies operating in the market: Etisalat, MobiNil, and Vodafone. The latter is a joint venture between Vodafone and Telecom Egypt, although reports surfaced in October 2013 that the wireless powerhouse is interested in selling its stake in the venture.

Egypt has 2.3 million broadband customers and a density rate of three percent.

Equatorial Guinea

Equatorial Guinea is one of the smallest countries in Africa, but it is also one of the wealthiest. Its per-capita GDP of $24,036 is the highest on the continent. There are 14,868 local access lines in the country, meaning two percent of the population has a landline telephone. The incumbent telecom operator, Getesa, had a monopoly on all landline services until competition was introduced in 2008. Today, the government owns 60 percent of the company while Orange holds the remaining 40 percent. A company called HiTs also operates in the local and long distance market.

The mobile density rate is 68 percent, with Orange and HiTs as the two providers. The broadband penetration rate is a very low 0.2 percent.

Eritrea

Erirea has the least developed telecommunications market in Africa. Less than one percent of the population has a landline telephone, the mobile density rate is less than five percent, and there are no broadband connections. Telecommunication Service of Eritrea has a monopoly on all telecommunications services. The company says it is investing in its infrastructure to provide Internet access and 3G wireless, but the country really needs outside investment to push growth. In 2013, the government attempted to jumpstart investment in the market by allowing Eritrean citizens to buy shares in the company. 

Ethiopia

In Ethiopia, a monopoly exists in nearly all telecom sectors and total telephone line penetration remains a very low 0.87 percent. In December 2010, the government ceded the management of Ethiopian Telecommunications Corporation (ETC), now known as Ethio Telecom, to the French Telecom operator Orange; the French company can more accurately meet the increasing communication demands of the company. It is unknown whether this step is a prelude to privatize the sector in general.

The mobile sector has been growing by 100 percent or more per annum in recent years, but the initial number was so small that the market is still small compared to the rest of the region. At the end of 2012, there were 20.5 million customers and a density rate of 22 percent. In November 2013, Ethio Telecom awarded a contract to Huawei Technologies for a 4G network in the capital city of Addis Ababa.

There are only 6,772 broadband connections among a population of 91.7 million people.

Gabon

Gabon is one of the wealthiest nations in Africa thanks to large oil reserves, but there there is a large class gap between wealthy and poor people in the country. Like many developing nations, wireless technologies have really taken off. Gabon was one of the first countries in Africa to have a wireless density rate over 100 percent. At the end of 2012, the mobile wireless density rate was 179 percent, while only one percent of the population has a landline telephone.

The telecom market has been open to competition since 1999, but Gabon Telecom is the major provider of local and long distance service. Maroc Telecom, which is owned by Vivendi, owns 51 percent of the company. A lack of any real competition has stifled investment and kept prices high. There are four mobile operators in Gabon: Airtel, Zaur, Libertis, and Moov.

Gambia

Gambia is the smallest country on mainland Africa. According to the most recent data published by the ITU, 3.5 percent of the population has a landline telephone, which is higher than many countries in Africa. Gambia Telecommunications Company (Gamtel) is the sole fixed-line provider, but Gambia is trying to improve its infrastructure through a project called the ECOWAS wide area network (ECOWAN). The network includes an 8,000 kilometer fiber-optic backbone and will use WiMAX or LTE technology to bring service to end users.

Gambia has 1.5 million wireless customers (an 85 percent density rate), and the country's four wireless carriers are Africell, Gamtel, Comium, and Qcell.

Ghana

Ghana has two national operators and a third regional operator, as well as four established mobile network providers and a fifth new mobile network. Zain entered the mobile market in Ghana in 2008 providing 3G services. In 2008, a record 3.8 million new subscribers were added to the market. Currently, Zain and MTN are the two dominant players in African telecommunications; MTN has a market share of 50 percent and is increasing. National and international public data services are provided by about 45 licensed companies and there are more than 130 VSAT networks. The Internet sector is served by more than 140 ISPs. Broadband ADSL services were introduced in 2003. Vodafone took over ownership of Ghana Telecom and the company has performed extensive network expansions and increased coverage. The full legalization of VoIP telephony and the implementation of broadband over power lines are expected soon. The two national operators (Ghana Telecom and Westel) held a duopoly on international service until February 2002. Zain rolled out 3.5G services throughout the country. The second national operator (SNO) Westel, which also holds a mobile license, sold 75 percent of its shares to Zain in 2007 for $120 million, but the government remains a 25 percent shareholder.

Bharti Airtel, the fifth leading global telecommunications company, officially launched their brand in Ghana in November 2010. The company spent $200 million to expand its operations in the country and bring in global partners; in addition, the operator partnered with world-class telecommunications infrastructure suppliers to ensure Airtel is the best quality network by 2015.

Ghana's population was 24.4 million in 2010. Penetration rates for telecom sectors in 2011 were 1 percent for fixed lines, 80 percent for mobile, and 11 percent for Internet. 

Guinea

Guinea is a wealthy nation because of vast mineral deposits, but its riches do not trickle down to the citizens. Its per-capita GDP is just $492, and the telecommunications sector does not fare much better. Only 0.2 percent of the population have a landline telephone, and 0.01 percent have a high-speed Internet connection. The monopoly service provider, Societe des Telecoms de Guinee (Sotelgui), has been marred with several problems. Telekom Malaysia formerly owned 60 percent of the company, but it sold its stake back to the Guinea government in 2005 for just $1. The federal government is looking to restructure and sell part of the company to a foreign investor in order to stabilize its finances. It has been kept afloat with loans from the World Bank. In January 2013, the government announced plans to build out a 4,000 kilometer fiber-optic backbone across the country.

There are four wireless carriers in Guinea: Cellcom, Intercel, MTN, and Orange. Together, they provide service to 4.8 million people (42 percent of the population).

Guinea-Bissau

Guinea-Bissau has a poor telecommunications sector. Only 0.3 percent of the population has a landline telephone and 63 percent have wireless phones. There are no broadband connections. The local and long distance markets are still a monopoly dominated by Guine Telecom. Portugal Telecom used to own 40 percent of the company but it sold its stake in 2010 and the government has been looking for another partner since. After several attempts to sell off part of Guine Telecom, the government started fielding offers for 80 percent of the company in November 2013.

Three mobile companies provide service to the country's one million wireless customers: Guinetel, MTN, and Orange.

Kenya

Kenya was expected to privatize Telkom Kenya in 2006, but did not complete the program until late 2007 with the sale of a 51 percent stake to a group led by France Telecom. The Kenyan government announced plans to sell its 60 percent stake, after restructuring. However, new carrier licenses introduced into the Internet sector enabled competition in the critical markets of VoIP telephony, wireless broadband, and ADSL, with ADSL2 and WiMAX to offer triple-play services. Major pan-Africa ISP Africa Online Limited introduced a mobile high-speed internet service in Kenya, making it only the second country after South Africa to have such a service. Kenya remains one of the fastest growing markets in Africa, but civil disturbances after the disputed 2008 elections brought political unrest to a country with a history of stability. 

Kenya has 30.7 million wireless customers, a density rate of 71 percent. There are four wireless carriers here: Airtel, Safaricom, Orange, and yu. In order to promote the development of 4G services, the government is considering allowing the carriers to share wireless spectrum.

Several Internet providers compete for market share, including Vocaltec, Access Kenya, and Telkom Kenya. In 2010, the secretary of Kenya's Ministry of Information and Communications announced that bringing reliable Internet services to Kenya was a high priority; in December of that year, fiber optic cables had been cut and dug up in the middle of the night in an act recognized as sabotage. Telkom Kenya announced in March 2010 that it would shift its focus to provide broadband connectivity to Kenyans at speeds of up to 8 Mbps. Airtel Kenya replaced the Zain brand name in November 2010 with a promise of high-quality customer benefits.

Penetration rates for telecom sectors in 2012 were 0.6 percent for fixed lines, 72 percent for mobile, and 0.1 percent for broadband Internet. 

Lesotho

Lesotho is a small landlocked enclave that is surrounded by South Africa. All sectors of the telecom market are open to competition, but the industry still trails several other African nations. The fixed-line density rate is just 2.5 percent. The primary fixed-line provider is Telecom Lesotho. The mobile sector is more advanced than the landline one. There are 1.6 million subscribers, a 75 percent density. There are three carriers operating there: Econoet, Teleo-Com, and Vodacom.

Just 0.7 percent of the population has a broadband connection. While the high-speed Internet market is poor, Lesotho is benefitting from a number of submarine fiber-optic projects connecting several African countries on the east and west coast with the rest of the world.

Liberia

Liberia was ravaged by two civil wars between 1989 and 2003. From 1997 to 2003, the country was also run by notorious warlord President Charles Tailor, who is now in prison for war crimes and crimes against humanity. All this strife has left the telecom market in an absolute shambles. The ITU reported there were only 10 local access lines in service at the end of 2012 and only 100 broadband customers. The government has tried to privatize Libtelco, the landline provider, but it did not attract any interest. In 2013, Liberian president Ellen Sirleaf suspended the company's entire board of directors for violating national procurement laws.

Wireless technologies have helped bring service to people who cannot get a landline telephone because of the decimated infrastructure. There are now 2.4 million wireless customers (a 57 percent rate), and there are four companies in the market: Atlantic Wireless, CellCom, Novafone, and Lonestar Cell, which is owned by MTN. Cellcom offers HSPA+ service in several major markets.

Libya

The Libyan telecommunications market is working to recover from the 2011 civil war when more than $1 billion in telecommunications infrastructure was destroyed. The telecommunications market was a complete monopoly under the Gaddafi regime, where the General Post and Telecommunication Company was responsible for local and long distance calling, while two state-owned companies operated in the mobile sector: Al Amdar Telecom Company and Libyana Mobile Phone. The new government is working on a new telecommunications policy that will create an independent regulatory authority and hopes to eventually introduce competition. The government also hopes to privatize one of the mobile carriers and issue another license in 2014.

Even though much of the country's infrastructure was damaged in the civil war, Libya still has one of the more advanced networks in the region. The country has a nationwide fiber-optic backbone and it is connected to two international cable systems. Libya is also one of the only countries in Europe to offer any sort of direct-fiber access.

Madagascar

Madagascar's telecom sector is independently regulated and has competition in mobile telephony. The national operator, Telecom Malagasy, has been privatized and in August 2013 Vodacom and MTN were both rumored to be interested in buying the company. National teledensity remains a low 1.1 percent, however.

The bulk of telephone connections are provided by the mobile networks. There are 8.8 million customers, a 40 percent density. The two main providers are Airtel and Orange.

The broadband infrastructure is very poor. Only 0.04 percent of the population has a high-speed Internet connection. In February 2013, Telecom Malagasy announced plans to install another 2,000 kilometers of fiber-optic cable to expand its nationwide backbone.

Malawi

With a per-capital GDP of just $268, Malawi is one of the least developed countries in the world. As to be expected, the country's telecommunications statistics are as poor as its citizens. Only one percent of the population has a landline telephone, 29 percent have mobile phones, and 0.02 percent have broadband connections. Most people cannot afford basic telephone service or Internet access. Making things worse, the government started taxing Internet service in 2013 and those charges were passed along to consumers.

There are two wireless Companies in Malawi: Airtel and Telekom Networks Malawi. A third company named G-Mobile was supposed to enter the market, but it delayed its launch so many times that the government revoked its license in 2013.

Mali

Mali ranks low on the Human Development Index and has a poor telecommunications infrastructure. At the end of 2012, only 0.8 percent of the population had a landline telephone and just 0.02 percent had a broadband Internet connection. The market has been open to competition since 2003, when Orange became the first company to compete against Sotel. The incumbent provider was privatized in 2009 when the government sold 51 percent of it to Maroc Telecom, which is owned by Vivendi of France.  

The two wireless carriers in Mali are Malitel and Orange, but a third company called Alpha Telecom Mali acquired a license in November 2013 and is preparing to launch service in 2014. It is a joint venture between Monaco Telecom and a local holding company.

Mauritania

Mauritania has a telecommunications market that is unique to Africa in that there are three companies that have licenses to provide service in both the landline and wireless sectors. They are Mauritel (the former monopolgy provider), Mattel (which is owned by Tunisie Telecom) and Chinguitel. Combined, they service 65,000 local access lines (1.7 percent of the population), four million mobile customers (29 percent density), and 6,697 broadband customers (0.18 percent of the population). One challenge for all three of these companies is a lack of access to inexpensive international bandwidth. Mauritania is connected to the rest of Africa through a fiber-optic cable through Senegal, but the incumbent provider there charges a steep price for bandwidth. In October 2013, the European Investment Bank announced that it was loaning the country $19.2 million to improve connectivity.

Mauritius

Mauritius is an island nation off of the southeast cost of Africa. It is home to 1.3 million people and one of the most advanced telecommunications markets in Africa. The penetration rates for fixed-line services (28 percent), mobile services (120 percent), and broadband Internet access (11 percent) are all among the highest in the region. Mauritius was also the first country in Africa to have nationwide Wi-MAX service, 3G wireless coverage, and IP television. It is also one of the few nations in Africa with 4G LTE service. Mauritius Telecom is still the main provider of local and long distance services, but Orange now owns 40 percent of it. The French telecom giant has played a key role in helping the company build out its network and offer new services.

The three wireless carriers are Orange, Emtel, and Mokoze.

Morocco

Morocco is considered to be one of the most advanced telecommunications markets in Africa. The country instituted substantial reforms in the 1990s, almost completely privatizing the incumbent fixed-line carrier, Maroc Telecom, and opening the industry to two additional competitors. The mobile market is also extremely competitive. As such, the sector as a whole has experienced tremendous growth. Even the fixed-line infrastructure has expanded at a healthy rate. According to the International Telecommunications Union, Morocco has 3.3 million fixed lines, a 10 percent density rate. The state-owned PTT is still the main provider. Full competition also exists in VSATs, data and Internet services, cybercafes, telecenters, and public call offices. Triple-play service offerings are being made available and WiMAX networks are being deployed. In November 2013, Etisalat bought Maroc Telecom for 4.2 billion euros, giving the Egyptian carrier a strong foothold in the country.

Morocco has 39 million wireless carriers, which is a 120 percent density. The country's wireless carriers are Maroc Telecom, Inwi, and Medi Telecom. 4G services are expected to be introduced in 2014.

The broadband rate is just 2.1 percent. The country's fixed-line infrastructure is being upgraded to support high-speed service as part of the country's National Broadband Plan, which aims to offer broadband service to everyone in the country by 2022.

Mozambique

Mozambique has been plagued by civil war, disease, famine, and flooding in recent years. It remains one of the least developed markets in the largely underdeveloped sub-Saharan Africa region. Recently, radical reforms have transformed the country into one of the fastest-growing economies on the continent, however. It was one of the first countries in the region to begin telecom reform, as far back as 1992. The sector is independently regulated and a second mobile license was awarded to Vodacom in mid-2002. The government-owned legacy telco, TDM, was converted into a limited company in May 2003, and its mobile subsidiary mCel was spun off. The fixed-line sector is held down by stagnant growth and high operating costs. At the end of 2012, Mozambique has a fixed-line density rate of just 0.4 percent.

The mobile area, however, has seen triple-digit growth rates almost every year since 1997 and there now 9.1 million customers, for a density of 36 percent. The main providers are Telecomunicacoes de Mozambique, VM SARL, and Vodacom.

The broadband rate is just 0.8 percent, but the government is working on building out a nationwide fiber-optic backbone.

Namibia

Namibia's fixed-line telecom market is still a monopoly, with Telecom Namibia operating all of the country's 171,249 local access lines (7.6 percent of the market). The government, however, plans to open the market to competition in order to meet its obligations as a member of the World Trade Organization (WTO).

The state of the wireless market is a little more modern, even though that part of the market was a monopoly up until 2007. At the end of 2012, the country had 2.1 million customers, meaning 95 percent of the country has a mobile phone. There are two wireless carriers in the country - MTC and TN Mobile - and they both offer advanced services. TN Mobile, a division of the Telecom Namibia, offers 3G service in parts of the country and is planning to build out a LTE network. MTC offers LTE service in the capital city of Windhoek.

Slightly more than one percent of the population has a high-speed broadband connection. There are six ISPs competing in the market, but growth has been hampered by a lack of inexpensive international bandwidth.

Niger

Niger is the largest country in west Africa, but 80 percent of its 1.27 million square kilometers of space is covered by the Sahara desert. Most of the country's population lives in the western and southern parts of the country, with only 0.6 percent of the population having a landline telephone. The country's infrastructure is old and mostly concentrated around major cities. The federal government voted in 2011 to privatize incumbent service provider Sonitel in an attempt to boost competition, but it reversed course a year later and nationalized it instead.

There are four companies servicing the 31 percent of the population that have mobile phones. They are Airtel, Orange, SahelCom, and Moov.

Only 0.02 percent of the population has broadband Internet subscriptions.

Nigeria

Nigeria has one of the fastest-growing telecommunications markets in Africa. The market is very liberalized and penetration rates are low, so foreign investors have been pouring into the country. Since liberalization began, competition has been introduced in the mobile market, and a second national operator (SNO) and four national long-distance operators have been licensed.  Over 200 companies have been licensed to provide value-added services, community and rural telephony, as well as regional and national telecom services. 

At the end of 2012, the fixed-line density rate was 0.25 percent. In July 2013, the federal government announced plans to liquidate Nitel, the state-owned landline provider, and its MTel wireless subsidiary because the company has been bankrupt for several years and has $2.2 billion in debt. Also, the government has been unable to privatize it.

In the mobile sector, triple-digit growth rates have occurred every year since 2002. The country is one of the fastest growing markets in Africa with the subscriber base in 2012 of more than 112 million (a 69 percent density rate). The carriers are Airtel, Etisalat, Glbacomm, Starcomms, MTN, MTel, Multilinks, Visafone, and ZoomMobile.

In the Internet area, the NCC has licensed more than 400 ISPs, although not all are operational.

Republic of the Congo

The Republic of the Congo has a telecommunications infrastructure that the CIA World Fact Book describes as "barely adequate for government use." Only 0.34 percent of the population has a landline telephone, and 0.01 percent has a broadband Internet connection. Most service is confined to within major cities as the lines connecting them are frequently out of service. These lines are operated by Socities Telecommunications du Congo (Congo Telecom).

The wireless sector is much more advanced. There are 4.3 million mobile customers for a density rate of 99 percent, and the four wireless carriers are Azur, Airtel, MTN Congo, and Warid.

Rwanda

Rwanda's telecommunications market was damaged during years of civil war and it was further hampered by poor infrastructure and high prices. As a result, the fixed-line density rate is just 0.4 percent and only 0.02 percent of the population have a landline telephone. The government is hoping to address this by building out a nationwide fiber-optic backbone network that will connect to submarine networks along the east coast of Africa. 

In the wireless market, there are 5.7 million subscribers (a density rate of 50 percent). Airtel, MTN, and Tigo are the three providers.

Sao Tome and Principe

Sao Tome and Principe is an island nation in the Gulf of Guinea. A company called CST is the monopoly provider of all telecom services. At the end of 2012, it had 8,035 land lines (4.27 density rate), 122,172 mobile customers (66 percent), and 778 broadband customers (0.41 percent).

Senegal

Senegal has one of Africa's most extensive and modern telecommunications infrastructures, with an above-average urban system with a fiber optic network. Liberalization began in 1997 with partial privatization of Sonatel. Mobile services were introduced in 1996 and competition was opened in 1999. The incumbent's monopoly officially ended in 2004, and a second national operator (SNO) and third mobile operator were licensed in 2006.  Since then, the number of mobile subscribers has grown, now representing more than 93 percent of all telephone lines. Internet usage has doubled every year since 2003 when ADSL services were introduced. Overall market penetration is still very low, however. Nearly two-thirds of all fixed-line connections are in Dakar, which is an emerging location within the call center industry.

Senegal has 11.5 million wireless customers, a density rate of 84 percent. The three wireless carriers are Orange, Tigo, and Expresso.

Seychelles

Seychelles is an archipelago of 115 islands off the southeast cost of Africa near Madagascar. Cable & Wireless is the main telecom provider and operates most of the 21,000 local access lines in the country. Wireless services are very popular here, with a density rate of 148 percent. The country's broadband rate of 11 percent is also one of the highest in the region.

Sierra Leone

Sierra Leone's telecommunications infrastructure was damaged during the ten years of civil war. At the end of 2012, there were just 18,000 local access lines for a teledensity rate of 0.3 percent. Sierra Leone Telecommunications Company is the main provider and the government is looking to privatize it.

In the mobile market, there are 2.2 million customers for a density rate of 37 percent, and there are a handful of companies operating there including Africell, Airtel, Comium Sierra Leone, and the Sierra Leone Telecommunications Company. In January 2014, the federal government threatened to seize Comium Sierra Leone because of ongoing problems with its network and its substantial debt load.

There is no broadband infrastructure. High-speed service was not available because, until 2013, the country relied entirely on satellites for international connections. In February 2013, the country linked up with a submarine fiber-optic cable that gave it access to more bandwidth and helped drop prices.

Somalia

Somalia has not had any regulatory structure since the government was overthrown in 1991. There are no regulations, service obligations, or taxes on service. While this has caused Somalia to have some of the lowest telecom rates in Africa, it has also created problems with carriers interconnecting as well as spectrum issues. At the end of 2012, just 0.7 percent of the population had basic telephone service. Hormund and Telsom are the two main providers.

The country also has 2.3 million mobile customers for a density rate of 23 percent. The wireless market is crowded: Golia Mobile, Hormuud Telecom, Nation Link, SOMAFONE, STC, Telecom Somalia, and Telesom Mobile Somalia all operate here.

Somalia does not have a broadband market because the threat of piracy off the coast has made it impossible for the country to connect to any submarine cable networks. As a result, it relies entirely on satellite connections for international bandwidth. In 2014, the rebel group Al-Shabaab ordered all Somali telecom carriers to ban Internet access or risk being attacked.

South Africa

South Africa has one of the most advanced and modern telecommunications networks in Africa. The much delayed and controversial awarding of a second national operator (SNO) license occurred in 2005 and launched in 2006 with Neotel. The first licenses for fixed-line services in under-serviced areas were awarded in 2004, opening competition in the market. A second round of licensing took place in 2005. Key centers within the country are Bloemfontein, Cape Town, Durban, Johannesburg, Port Elizabeth, and Pretoria.

Although its period of exclusivity ended in May 2002, Telkom SA remains the dominant provider of fixed-line telecommunications services in South Africa. Eskom and Transtel have a combined 30 percent stake in the new SNO. Another vendor, Sentech, operates an international gateway used by South African telcos and provides multimedia services. The roll out of Sentech's wireless broadband network was one of the South African government's three major public infrastructure programs over the past two years.

In the mobile sector, there are four networks operated by Vodacom, MTN, Cell C, and Telkom 8ta. The density rate is 131 percent, and the chief driver is prepaid service which now accounts for about 85 percent of all mobile users. Third generation (3G) services were introduced in December 2004. Cell C had a tough time entering a market dominated by Vodacom (58 percent market share and 23 million customers) and MTN (34.3 percent market share) and is still at less than 10 percent market share with about four million subscribers. South Africa's biggest cellular operator Vodacom has launched its long-awaited GPRS technology, packaged with advanced text messaging technology into a new product called My Life.

The data and Internet sector includes numerous licensed operators, and the market has an annual growth rate of more than 25 percent, stimulated by ADSL. Competition in the South African telecom industry received a major boost from the Electronic Communications Bill passed in 2006. The bill ended the state-sanctioned monopoly of Telkom and freed up the telecommunications, technology, and broadcasting markets. Meanwhile, Telkom introduced converged triple-play services. However, charges for both fixed-line and mobile calls in South Africa continue to be among the world's most costly. 3G mobile services have been introduced by Vodafone.

South Sudan

South Sudan is the youngest country in world, having seceded from Sudan in 2011. The telecom market suffered from decades of neglect and thus has some of the poorest statistics in the world. For example, the ITU reports that at the end of 2012, there were just 150 land lines and 17 broadband connections. South Sudan has a wireless density rate of 21 percent, which is one of the lowest in Africa. Zain is the leading telecom provider.

Further complicating things, an armed rebellion broke out in December 2012.

Sudan

Sudan has one of the fastest growing fixed telephony markets not only in Africa but also in the world. As a result, it attracts a large amount of foreign investment in the telecommunications sector. Still, the fixed density rate is just 1.1 percent, while the wireless rate is 74 percent. There are five carriers operating in the wireless market: Canar, MTN, Sudan Mobile Telephone Company, Vivacell, and Zain.

Broadband usage is also low at just 0.07 percent. ADSL services were introduced in 2004, a second mobile network commenced operations in July 2005, and a second fixed-line license was issued in November 2004. Wide cellular coverage is available in most major cities.

Swaziland

Swaziland's telecommunications market is still mostly a monopoly. The Swaziland Posts and Telecommunications Corporation (SPTC) is the only provider of local and long distance calling, and it operates all of the country's 45,565 local access lines. MTN is the only wireless provider and services all of the country's 805,000 customers. The government is, however, considering issuing a license to another wireless carrier.

Tanzania

Tanzania has a competitive telecom sector with four mobile networks (Vodacom, Celtel, Mobitel, and Zantel) and two fixed-line operators. The fixed-line operators initially covered the mainland and the semi-autonomous island of Zanzibar separately. The Internet sector has been very slow to take off. There was partial privatization of the incumbent in 2000 and its monopoly ended on the mainland in 2005. VoIP was liberalized under a new competition framework in 2005.

Tanzania Telecommunications Company Limited (TTCL) substantially reduced its telephone tariffs on May 1, 2006. TTCL launched a new land and mobile service in 2006 called TTCL Mobile Service. To boost access for telecommunications services in rural populations, Tanzanian officials have urged local telecommunications firms to increase investment in rural areas instead of just urban areas. According to the report, 80 percent of Tanzanians live in rural areas. The first fiber optic international submarine cables in the country in 2009 and 2010 changed the market, since it had formerly depended on satellite connections.

Togo

Togo's telecommunications market is like many others in Africa: a lack of infrastructure and a poor economy mean the density rates for landline and broadband service are both very low, while wireless technologies have helped bring basic telephone service to some of the population. According to the most recent information published by the ITU, Togo has 61,590 local access lines, meaning just 0.9 percent of the people have a landline telephone. The local calling market is open to competition, but Togo Telecom has a monopoly on all long distance services .

In the mobile sector, there are 3.3 million wireless customers for a 50 percent density. Moov and Togo Cellulaire are the two carriers.

Togo also has a poor broadband infrastructure. At the end of 2012, there were just 7,000 broadband customers, meaning only 0.1 percent of the population has a high-speed connection. In 2013, an article in a publication called BiztechAfrica criticzed Togo for having the most unstable and inconsistent Internet connectivity in Africa.

Tunisia

Tunisia has one of the more developed telecommunications infrastructures in Africa with a fixed-line teledensity of more than 12 percent; constant upgrades are being made to continue the development to meet the latest telecommunications standards. The mobile sector has seen a high rate of growth, with a second operator licensed in 2002. 3G was implemented in trial systems in September 2004. Internet access is available across the country through a fiber-optic backbone. International access is provided through submarine cables as well as terrestrial and satellite links. Privatization of Tunisie Telecom occurred in 2006, when Dubai Telecom purchased 35 percent of the company after several other contenders, such as Saudi Telecom and Telefonica, pulled out of the process. Key centers include Sfax, Sousse, Bizerte, and Tunis. The government awarded the concession to build and operate a VSAT network with international connectivity.

The Tunisian government awarded a 3G license to state-controlled telco Tunisie Telecom (TT) in September 2010 for $80 million, putting it in competition with France Telecom's local unit, Orange Tunisia, which launched a joint 2G/3G network in May. TT is 65 percent owned by the state, whereas Emirates International Telecommunications (EIT) owns the remainder. TT and Tunisiana, the local arm of Orascom Telecom, each control 48.7 percent of the market, while Orange had a 2.6 percent market share with 297,000 customers after two months of operation, launching Tunisia's first commercial 3G mobile service. Telco Tunicell was licensed in August 2011 as the second 3G mobile broadband service.

Uganda

As early as 1999, Uganda became one of the first countries in Africa where mobile subscribers passed fixed-line subscribers; the ratio is now over 50 to 1. However, the service and number of main fixed lines is inadequate. The fixed-line density rate is just 0.87 percent, while the wireless rate is 45 percent. Regardless, all market segments are experiencing strong growth.

Uganda was one of the first countries in the sub-Saharan region to gain full Internet connectivity. Both the incumbent Uganda Telecom (UTL) and MTN Uganda (owning 50 percent of the market) offer a range of data services. The introduction of UTL's Freenet service and a special Internet tariff countrywide have helped to increase Internet usage, as has the recent strong growth of the fixed-line networks. A new competition framework introduced in 2005 liberalized VoIP completely, and the incumbent monopoly ended in July 2005. Because the country is  landlocked, it was dependent on satellites for its international Internet connectivity; this ended in 2009 when Uganda connected to international submarine fiber optic cables, resulting in lower prices.

As of 2014, there are six wireless carriers operating here: Airtel, Orange, I-Tel, MTN Telecom, UTL Telecel, and Warid.

Zambia

Zambia's telecommunications sector has an independent regulator and is competitive in the mobile sector with three mobile operators. The fixed-line operator, Zamtel, remains a monopoly; the Libyan government owns a majority stake in Zamtel. Zambia has been engaged in a battle with the IMF and World Bank for several years to stave off privatization. Zambia's government is rejecting IFI pressure and plans to operate Zamtel on a commercial basis with a possible minority shareholder. Celtel Zambia ended Zamtel's international direct dialing (IDD) monopoly when its international gateway license was approved. The telephone network is at a very low level of development with fewer than 83,000 connected main lines for a population of more than 14 million. The mobile sector has seen high growth, as in most of the continent. The Zambia Telecommunications Company is the main provider.

Recently, moves have been made towards liberalization of the regulatory environment. High-capacity microwave relay technology allows telecommunications services to connect most larger towns and cities. VSAT networks are operated by private firms. Wireless technologies have also made much more headware, as there were 10.5 million customers at the end of 2012, a 75 percent density rate. Airel, MTN, and Zamtel are the wireless carriers.

Zimbabwe

Despite a serious and long lasting economic crisis, Zimbabwe's telecom sector has seen growth in both the mobile and Internet sectors. Currently, the outdated telecommunications infrastructure suffers from poor maintenance. Fixed-line service has shown some gradual improvement. According to the most recent information from the ITU, Zimbabwe has 301,560 landlines, a density of 2.2 percent.

Mobile penetration continues to rise at a much faster pace, partly due to the efforts of the three mobile networks: Econet, NetOne, and Telecel Zimbabwe. NetOne's parent, TelOne, is the fixed-line service monopoly holder, but the government reportedly plans to privatize up to 60 percent of TelOne and NetOne. Almost 92 percent of the population - 12.6 million people - have mobile phones.

Like most of the region, the broadband rate is very low. Only 0.5 percent of the people have high speed connections.

Market Trends

[return to top of this report]

A look forward first necessitates a look backward. The telecom sector in Africa has changed considerably since the early 1990s, in part due to liberalization, modernization, and privatization. While the development and expansion of infrastructure has not kept pace, for the most part, growth in areas such as mobile and Internet provide reason for optimism. There is a concerted multi-year effort by many organizations of varying size to help ease Africa's growing pains as it moves forward. These include the International Telecommunications Union, the World Trade Organization, the UN Economic Commission for Africa, the National Telephone Cooperative Association, and the Afrinet 2000 Summit, which was composed of eight West African States.

Mobile communications have taken off in recent years at an astonishing rate. Although mobile services still remain largely within the cities, they do provide some hope for service to outlying districts. The main thwarting factors are the costs of cell phone handsets and the high cost per minute - making them particularly unacceptable for applications such as Internet access until faster wireless services arrive. There is also a problem of ensuring a profit for carriers bringing cellular services to rural areas, since individuals have abysmally small pay rates. Some of this is being handled through subsidies, grants, and other devices, but development of better telecommunications services will inevitably require a general increase in prosperity. Ironically, improved communications are also the best avenue for fostering increased prosperity.

Satellite communication also offers potential to reach remote African villages, perhaps some day at a rate cheaper than other services. Once thought certain to be Africa's eventual solution, satellite communication ventures are now finding increased and unexpected competition from cellular access. It still remains an important option. A key initiative in this area is the Regional African Satellites Communications (RASCOM) project, which aims to provide a continent-wide system of satellite communications. RASCOM is an inter-governmental initiative founded in 1992, and now having 44 members. Implementation is being handled by Alcatel. Progress with this project has been slow, with the first pan-African satellite being launched into orbit in late December 2007, more than one year after its planned implementation date.

Some mobile carriers are converging licenses to enter new service sectors and rolling out national fiber optic backbone networks. Most African nations offer 3G somewhere within the country, but only a couple offer 4G service. In the Internet sector, the number of broadband users is lagging behind the worldwide average. Broadband penetration is under one percent in many countries, but the lack of development so far represents huge potential. According to TeleGeography's Global Bandwidth Forecast Service, Africa's demand for international bandwidth is expected to grow by a compound annual growth rate of 51 percent between 2012 and 2019. The report continued to say most of this demand will come from sub-Shara Africa.

Developments in the telecommunications sector have come in incremental rather than large steps. Progress has also been more pronounced in some countries than in others, and the onrush of mobile services has somewhat changed the equation. The lowest overall teledensity is found in Ethiopia and Eritrea, where it remains at slightly more than two phones per 100 people. On the other hand, Mauritius and the Seychelles Islands both have teledensities in excess of 100 percent. It is important to note, however, that these figures do not take into account the vast rural areas of all of these countries, where even basic access to telephone service in any form remains problematic. In Seychelles, Bharti Airtel announced a $10 million investment over the next three years. In addition, to support the large number of corporate and government Internet users, Seychelles will announce a 2000 kilometer undersea fiber optic cable in less than two years.

Mobile phone and Internet uptake continue to grow rapidly throughout the region. Some estimates conclude that mobile users comprise over 90 percent of all African telephone subscribers. However, as lower income groups are being targeted, a price-sensitive market for lower-cost fixed or limited-mobility services is emerging.In 2004, the last two countries on the continent, Guinea-Bissau and Eritrea, launched mobile services. Africa was the first continent in which mobile phones outnumbered fixed telephone lines. For both voice and data services, wireless technologies are increasingly substituting for fixed-line infrastructures, which remain in poor condition. The first 3G systems were launched in Africa in March 2003 with CDMA-2000 and in November 2004 for W-CDMA.

Mergers and acquisitions across the telecom sector within Africa are popular. In South Africa, values were down at least 50 percent compared with other emerging markets, but much activity occurred in 2008 and 2009. The largest deal in South Africa was Vodafone's purchase of a 15 percent stake in Vodacom for $2.3 billion, which brings the total holding to 65 percent. In December 2008, Vodafone purchased African network and satellite services firm Gateway Communications for $700 million. Pan-African company MTN Group acquired Verizon's businesses, with a purchase of a 69.38 percent stake in Verizon South Africa. Also, Kuwait-based Zain promises a major African expansion by acquiring smaller players within the market.

Mobile services have become an important part of Africa's development and the increasing penetration rates prove this. Operators are starting to create the necessary infrastructure to facilitate the offering of services such as short message sending, data transmission, and WAP-based Internet access. More will have to be done if Africa is to improve its overall telecom industry.

There is one major drawback to development, however. Most of the population has only a basic elementary school education, with 30 percent of men and 50 percent of women considered illiterate. The level of education is of concern to potential telecom operators who need skilled personnel to maintain on-going high-tech businesses. High-tech businesses are expected to create 500,000 jobs in the next few years. More firms are expected to look to Africa for lower salaried but skilled data processing employees.

Wireless

According to the latest data from the CIA World Factbook, over 42 percent of the total population of just more than 1 billion are mobile users. The key mobile markets are found within the following 6 African countries: South Africa, Nigeria, Morocco, Egypt, Algeria, and Kenya. The mobile penetration rate in South Africa is much higher than the average, close to 90 percent. Although there has been an extremely high rate of growth in cellular services, much of it has been confined to metropolitan areas, with some individuals (particularly in Nigeria) having as many as four cell phones to ensure service connection. This can make actual dispersion estimates unreliable. Increasing actual penetration rates in rural areas will require the investment of huge sums into the continent's overall telecom sector Another hindrance is unclear or complicated regulations that have failed to provide sufficient reassurances to potential investors. In some countries, investment is also discouraged by lack of privatization and monopolistic practices. Nonetheless, cellular services have seen spectacular growth, with the market exploding by a factor of several thousand since 1998. Cellular is also overtaking wireline in most sectors, for not only access and economic reasons but also because the voice signal is often clearer than that provided by antiquated and poorly maintained copper.

Growth in the mobile sector is fueled by Foreign investments; almost all countries are currently experiencing massive growth in mobile subscribers. By 2015, it is projected that mobile subscriptions in Africa will surpass 840 million. Already, at least 15 operators have announced that they will expand to 3G voice and data services in countries such as Tanzania, Kenya, and Nigeria. Individuals in Africa are using SMS capabilities to obtain pricing information for agricultural products, conduct mobile banking, and notify authorities of human rights abuses. Cellular phones, which are really miniature computers, are essential in these areas.

Part of the reason for the growing use of mobile telephones is that many Africans could not afford a laptop or desktop; thus, Internet services are out of reach for many individuals. Other barriers to Internet use include low-income groups, high taxation for these products, and widespread illiteracy. Many human rights groups are implementing "individual empowerment" to overcome some of these obstacles. Price wars in some markets with a lot of carriers are causing prices to fall steeply, bringing access to more and more Africans.

Internet

The leaders of the African nations recognize the important role of the Internet and are taking steps to jump start subscription rates. Internet usage, however, is dependent on connectivity and PC usage, and PC usage remains low to nonexistent outside of the cities. All countries in the region have Internet access at least in capital cities, however. There are 250 or more Points of Presence (PoPs) across the continent, with the vast majority in South Africa. Several countries have expanding ADSL or other broadband access infrastructures. Cybercafes and telecenters remain the sole point of access for many.

In the past few years fiber connectivity was built under the ocean to expand the lacking Internet connectivity within Africa. The implications of this infrastructure change are monumental. First, new fiber networks can improve the ability of businesses to forge relationships with global partners; better connectivity can open new opportunities for communications and information technologies. 

In October 2010, Orange mobile introduced affordable Internet access for prepaid customers; the company plans to introduce 3G services on the GSM platform by the end of 2010. Satellite operator SES will expand its presence in Africa and add staff to African offices. The company plans to launch five satellites in four years; each satellite can support digital television. This solution aids rural Africans, who do not have access to basic cable infrastructure. The new service will handle formats including digital HDTV, 3DTV, and IP.

According to recent projections, it is expected that Eritrea and Western Sahara will be the only two African countries without access to a data cable. It is expected that decreasing data costs could fuel business and increase access to reliable info.
